Drug overdose deaths Overdose deaths grew by nearly 44 percent between 2009 and 2015, with much of that increase driven by the growth in the number of deaths due to opioids. (Sources: 1, 2) 36,450 (2008) 52,404 (2015)
Opioid deaths In just six years, the number of deaths due to opioid abuse grew by nearly 69 percent, one measure of just how fast-growing this epidemic is and how major of a public health crisis this could become if left unchecked. (Sources: 1, 2) 19,582 (2008) 33,091 (2015)
Obesity rate While the rate of obesity has stabilized at about 17 percent in children and adolescents, the rate for adults has continued to go up, despite major public health initiatives to reverse the adult trend. (Values shown are the share of adults age 20 or older who are obese. | Sources: 1, 2) 33.7% (2007-2008) 37.7% (2013-2014)
Life expectancy Life expectancy has gone up slowly but surely in this period — the latest for which official statistics have been completed. But preliminary data for 2015 suggest that life expectancy dipped slightly, by about a month, in 2015. This may be a one-time blip or the beginning of a trend. The 2014 data show that life expectancy for non-Hispanic whites went down by about a month (0.1 year) but did not affect the overall 2014 rate, which was unchanged from 2013. (Sources: 1, 2) 78.1 years (2008) 78.8 years (2014)
Any past illicit drug use, 12th grade Overall, teenagers’ use of drugs, alcohol and tobacco are at their lowest rates since the 1990s, according to the annual Monitoring the Future survey. Any marijuana/hashish use ever, however, continued to rise in 2016 and stood at 44.5 percent. (Sources: 1, 2) 47.4% (2008) 48.3% (2016)
Ever been drunk, 12th grade Annual and monthly prevalence of alcohol use are at the lowest rates in decades. Binge drinking has fallen by half or more at each grade level since peaking at the end of the 1990s, according to the Monitoring the Future survey. (Sources: 1, 2) 54.7% (2008) 46.3% (2016)
Any cigarette use, 12th grade Smoking cigarettes has dropped off substantially in recent years among all youth. At the same time, the rate of vaping has increased and is now reported by 14 percent of 12th-graders. There is continued debate over whether vaping represents a “safer” form of smoking. (Sources: 1, 2) 44.7% (2008) 28.3% (2016)
